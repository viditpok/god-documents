Bankruptcy Prediction Report (1964–2020)

1. Data Overview

Firm-level accounting (Compustat) and market data (CRSP) were merged with bankruptcy filings. The merged dataset spans 1964–2023 and covers 18,682 firms across 203,065 firm-years, including 735 bankruptcy events. Lagged values were used to ensure ex-ante prediction.

Years Covered1964–2023

Bankruptcy Events735

2. Key Variables Used

The predictive model used firm-level measures of profitability, leverage, liquidity, and size:

VariableDescription
LeverageDebt-to-assets ratio capturing financial risk.
ROAProfitability indicator (net income / assets).
Current RatioLiquidity measure of short-term solvency.
Profit MarginOperational profitability (net income / sales).
Asset TurnoverEfficiency in asset utilization.
Log AssetsFirm size proxy.
Firm AgeYears since first observation for the firm.

4. Empirical Findings

KNN (K=20) achieved the strongest out-of-sample performance with an AUC of 0.632, accuracy of 0.996, and a misclassification rate of 0.004.

Across model families:

  • Regularized logistic models (LASSO, Ridge) outperform standard logistic in both AUC and stability.
  • KNN performs moderately but struggles with class imbalance.
  • Tree-based methods (RF, XGBoost, LightGBM) show significant improvement, confirming nonlinearity in predictors.
  • Survival RF introduces timing elements, performing slightly below gradient-boosted methods but capturing temporal risk structure.

5. Data-Driven Insights

  • Leverage and profitability ratios consistently emerge as dominant predictors of bankruptcy.
  • Firm size (log assets) and liquidity (current ratio) provide strong early-warning indicators.
  • Across ensemble models, feature importances confirm leverage and ROA as the two leading risk determinants.
  • The top decile of predicted probabilities captures a disproportionate share of actual bankruptcies (high concentration ratio).

6. Summary

The combination of financial ratios, size, and age produces robust bankruptcy predictions. Boosted ensemble methods—particularly XGBoost and LightGBM—achieve the best balance between accuracy and interpretability. The final results reflect the advantage of nonlinear modeling in capturing firm distress dynamics.
